It started with a joro spider in the backyard.

I'd seen them around before — those massive, brightly colored orb-weavers that showed up in the southeast a few years ago. Originally from East Asia, *Trichonephila clavata* has been making itself at home in Georgia and the Carolinas, spinning enormous golden webs between trees, power lines, and basically anything that stays still long enough.

One afternoon I saw one suspended in a web that caught the light just right, and I grabbed my phone to take a picture. The result was... fine. You could tell it was a spider. But it didn't capture what I was actually seeing — the intricate patterns on the abdomen, the almost metallic sheen of the legs, the way the silk threads caught individual droplets of dew.

That's what got me into macro photography.

## What is macro photography?

Macro photography is close-up photography, typically at 1:1 magnification or greater — meaning the subject is captured at life-size or larger on the camera sensor. It reveals a world that's invisible to the naked eye: the texture of a spider's exoskeleton, the compound structure of an insect's eye, the veins on a leaf backlit by morning sun.

## The gear rabbit hole

I started simple — a clip-on macro lens for my phone. That was enough to get me hooked but not enough to get the shots I wanted. I eventually picked up a dedicated macro lens, and suddenly I was seeing everything differently.

Every walk became an expedition. Every garden was a studio. I started noticing things I'd walked past a thousand times — tiny jumping spiders on the mailbox, iridescent beetles on the rosemary, caterpillars that looked like miniature dragons.

## Back to the joros

But I keep coming back to the joro spiders. They're big enough to photograph without needing extreme magnification, their coloring is stunning, and their webs are architectural feats. They're patient subjects too — they tend to sit still in the center of their webs, which makes framing a shot much easier than chasing a jumping spider across a wall.

If you're in the southeast US, keep an eye out for them in late summer and fall. They're hard to miss once you start looking.
